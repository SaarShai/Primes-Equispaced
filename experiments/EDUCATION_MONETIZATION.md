This portfolio represents a "Vertical Software Ecosystem." You are not just selling isolated products; you are building a stack where the **Library** (2) builds the user base, the **Textbook** (3) builds the authority, the **SaaS** (1) captures institutional budgets, and the **Platform** (4) captures the individual learner market.

Here is the breakdown of the four products:

### 1. Spectroscope Explorer (SaaS)
*The "Cash Flow" product for institutional budgets.*

*   **Revenue Estimate:** High variance. Success depends on "Departmental" vs. "University-wide" sales. At \$2k/year, you need 50 departments to hit \$100k. Because this sits in departmental budgets (Dean/Dept Head level), it avoids the grueling "University IT Procurement" cycle.
*   **Timeline:** **Long (12–18 months).** You need a stable MVP, a pilot program with one "prestige" university to gain social proof, and a polished UI.
*   **Effort:** **High (Engineering + Sales).** The effort isn't just coding; it’s managing uptime, security compliance (to satisfy universities), and the "boots on the ground" effort of reaching out to professors.

### 2. farey-tools (Open Core Library)
*The "Lead Magnet" and "Technical Moat."*

*   **Revenue Estimate:** Low/Medium. The "Free" tier acts as your marketing engine. Revenue comes from "Lab Licenses"—small grants that allow a PI (Principal Investigator) to buy the GPU/Batch features for their entire research group.
*   **Timeline:** **Short/Iterative (6–12 months).** You can release the core functionality almost immediately. Revenue follows as you release "Compute-intensive" modules.
*   **Effort:** **Medium (Optimization).** The effort is concentrated in highly specialized C++/CUDA/Python engineering to ensure the "Premium" features actually provide a massive speedup over the free version.

### 3. Textbook / Monograph
*The "Authority Builder."*

*   **Revenue Estimate:** Low. This is a "Prestige" product. It is unlikely to fund a company, but it is essential for establishing you as the global expert in Computational Farey Theory. This authority makes the SaaS and the Courses much easier to sell.
*   **Timeline:** **Very Long (24–36 months).** Writing, peer review, and the publishing cycle for academic monographs is notoriously slow.
*   **Effort:** **High (Intellectual/Deep Work).** This is "Deep Work" heavy. It requires intense concentration, mathematical rigor, and no "engineering" effort, but immense "intellectual" stamina.

### 4. Online Course Platform (Proprietary)
*The "Scalable Engine."*

*   **Revenue Estimate:** Very High. Unlike Udemy (where you take a small cut), owning the platform allows you to keep 95-97% of the margin. If you capture 1,000 students at \$100/course, that is \$100k with almost zero marginal cost per student.
*   **Timeline:** **Medium/Long (12–24 months).** You have a dual-track timeline: Building the LMS (Learning Management System) software AND producing high-quality, professional video/interactive content.
*   **Effort:** **Very High (Full Stack + Content).** This is the most difficult to scale. It requires software engineering (web/video streaming), instructional design (pedagogy), and aggressive digital marketing (SEO/Paid Ads) to drive traffic.

### The Strategy: The "Flywheel" Effect

To succeed, do not view these as four separate businesses. View them as one **Engine**:

1.  **The Library (2)** attracts the PhD students and Researchers.
2.  **The Textbook (3)** convinces them that your methods are the "Gold Standard."
3.  **The SaaS (1)** captures the budget of the Professors they work for.
4.  **The Platform (4)** captures the lifelong learners and undergrads who use the library and read the book.

**Recommendation on where to start:** Start with **Product 2 (Library)**. It has the lowest barrier to entry and builds the user base required to make Products 1 and 4 viable.
