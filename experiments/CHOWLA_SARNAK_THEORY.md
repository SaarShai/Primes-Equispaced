# Chowla, Sarnak, and the Pre-Whitened Mobius Spectroscope

## 0. Scope, conventions, and status labels

This note formalizes the relation between:

- the pre-whitened Mobius spectroscope
- Chowla-type correlation cancellation
- Sarnak-style Mobius orthogonality to deterministic dynamics

Throughout, `\mu(n)` is the Mobius function, `M(x) = \sum_{n \le x} \mu(n)` is the Mertens function, and `\rho = \beta + i\gamma` ranges over the nontrivial zeros of `\zeta(s)`.

I use the following labels.

- `PROVED`: a standard theorem or a direct consequence of a standard theorem.
- `CONDITIONAL`: a statement conditional on RH, simplicity of zeros, Chowla, or a standard probabilistic proxy for Chowla.
- `HEURISTIC`: a model-based statement, not a theorem.
- `COMPUTATIONAL`: a definition or method intended for numerical work, not a theorem.

The main technical correction is the following.

- For the weighted transform defined below, the natural amplitude scale is `O(log N)` and the natural power scale is `O((log N)^2)`.
- Therefore a peak threshold such as `N^{2-\epsilon}` belongs to the *unweighted* Fourier transform `\sum_{n \le N} \mu(n) e^{-i\gamma \log n}`, not to the pre-whitened transform `\sum_{n \le N} \mu(n) n^{-1} e^{-i\gamma \log n}`.

That scaling issue is corrected explicitly below.

---

## 1. The pre-whitened Mobius spectroscope

### 1.1 Definition

For `N \ge 1` define

```math
S_N(\gamma) := \sum_{n \le N} \frac{\mu(n)}{n} e^{-i \gamma \log n}
            = \sum_{n \le N} \frac{\mu(n)}{n^{1+i\gamma}},
```

and

```math
F_N(\gamma) := |S_N(\gamma)|^2.
```

This is the pre-whitened Mobius spectroscope. The factor `1/n` makes the coefficient sequence square-summable, so the natural background power is finite.

### 1.2 Explicit formula for `M(x)`

#### Proposition 1.1 (Perron explicit formula; standard)

Let `x > 1` and `T \ge 2`. Then

```math
M(x)
= \frac{1}{2\pi i} \int_{c-iT}^{c+iT} \frac{x^s}{s \zeta(s)} \, ds
  + O\!\left(\frac{x^c}{T}\right),
  \qquad c>1.
```

After shifting the contour left and collecting residues, one obtains

```math
M(x)
= -2
  + \sum_{|\Im \rho| \le T} \frac{x^\rho}{\rho \zeta'(\rho)}
  + \sum_{m \ge 1} \frac{x^{-2m}}{-2m\, \zeta'(-2m)}
  + E_T(x),
```

with an error term `E_T(x)` coming from the shifted contour. Under RH and simple zeros, one has the standard truncation

```math
M(x) = -2 + \sum_{\rho} \frac{x^\rho}{\rho \zeta'(\rho)} + \text{(trivial-zero terms)} + \text{(smaller terms)}.
```

#### Proof sketch

Apply Perron's formula to `1/\zeta(s)` and shift the contour. The pole at `s=0` contributes `-2` because `1/\zeta(0) = -2`. The nontrivial zeros contribute residues `x^\rho / (\rho \zeta'(\rho))` when the zeros are simple. The trivial zeros at `s=-2m` contribute the remaining explicit terms. Standard references are Titchmarsh and Iwaniec-Kowalski.

#### Remark 1.2

The user-supplied shorthand `M(x) = -2 + \sum_\rho x^\rho/\rho + \cdots` is the same formula with the residue factors `1/\zeta'(\rho)` absorbed into the coefficients. I keep the canonical residue normalization `1/(\rho \zeta'(\rho))` because it is the correct explicit-formula coefficient.

### 1.3 From `M(x)` to the spectroscope

Write

```math
S_N(\gamma)
= \int_{1^-}^{N} x^{-1-i\gamma}\, dM(x)
= N^{-1-i\gamma} M(N) + (1+i\gamma)\int_1^N M(x)\, x^{-2-i\gamma}\, dx.
```

Insert one residue term `x^\rho / (\rho \zeta'(\rho))` from `M(x)`. A routine partial-summation calculation shows that the leading response of the zero `\rho_k = 1/2 + i\gamma_k` is

```math
\frac{1+i\gamma}{\rho_k \zeta'(\rho_k)} \cdot \frac{1}{1/2 + i(\gamma-\gamma_k)}
+ O(N^{-1/2})
```

uniformly for `\gamma` in compact sets away from the other zero ordinates. The exact finite-N expression contains a boundary factor `1-N^{-1/2-i(\gamma-\gamma_k)}`; I suppress it here because it is lower order for the asymptotic peak shape.

After collecting the deterministic prefactors and keeping the leading frequency response, the peak generated by the zero ordinate `\gamma_k` is modeled by a Lorentzian kernel.

#### Definition 1.3 (spectral coefficient and kernel)

Let

```math
c_k := \frac{2(1+i\gamma_k)}{\rho_k \zeta'(\rho_k)}
      = \frac{2(1+i\gamma_k)}{(1/2+i\gamma_k)\zeta'(1/2+i\gamma_k)}.
```

Define the normalized kernel

```math
K(u) := \frac{1}{1+4u^2},
```

so that `K(0)=1`.

Then the leading zero contribution to the power spectrum is

```math
F_N(\gamma)
\approx \sum_k |c_k|^2 K(\gamma-\gamma_k)
```

up to interference terms between distinct zeros and a finite-cutoff error of size `O(N^{-1/2+\varepsilon})` in amplitude under the usual RH-based truncation heuristics.

#### Remark 1.4

If RH fails, then the same formula still holds with `\rho = \beta + i\gamma` and the peak centers move to the ordinates of *all* nontrivial zeros; the critical-line notation `\gamma_k` is then only a special case.

### 1.4 The residual

Define the residual

```math
R_N(\gamma) := F_N(\gamma) - \sum_k |c_k|^2 K(\gamma-\gamma_k).
```

This is the object whose behavior should be compared with white noise after the deterministic zero peaks are removed.

#### Proposition 1.5 (dimensionally correct upper bound)

For every `\gamma \in \mathbb R`,

```math
|S_N(\gamma)| \le \sum_{n \le N} \frac{1}{n}
= \log N + \gamma_E + O(1/N),
```

and therefore

```math
F_N(\gamma) \ll (\log N)^2.
```

In particular, any claim of a peak of size `N^{2-\epsilon}` is not compatible with the weighted spectroscope `F_N`; that scale belongs to the unweighted transform `|\sum_{n \le N}\mu(n)e^{-i\gamma\log n}|^2`.

#### Proof

The first inequality is the triangle inequality. The second is immediate by squaring.

## 2. Chowla, white noise, and the residual

### 2.1 What Chowla gives and what it does not

#### Chowla conjecture

For distinct shifts `h_1,\dots,h_k` and `\varepsilon_i \in \{1,2\}`, if at least one `\varepsilon_i = 1` then

```math
\sum_{n \le N} \mu^{\varepsilon_1}(n+h_1)\cdots \mu^{\varepsilon_k}(n+h_k) = o(N).
```

This is a deterministic conjecture about higher correlations of `\mu`.

#### Important caveat

Chowla by itself does not define an expectation operator. To compute `E[R_N(\gamma)]` and `Var(R_N(\gamma))` one must pass to a probabilistic proxy for Chowla.

### 2.2 The standard probabilistic proxy

#### HEURISTIC MODEL 2.1 (Cramer-Chowla proxy)

Treat the values `\mu(n)` as centered random signs with

```math
E[\mu(n)] = 0,
 \qquad
E[\mu(m)\mu(n)] = \delta_{m,n},
```

and higher moments factorizing up to the squarefree constraint.

This is the natural white-noise model suggested by Chowla. It is not a theorem.

### 2.3 First and second moments

Let

```math
T_N(\gamma) := \sum_{n > N} \frac{\mu(n)}{n} e^{-i\gamma \log n}
```

be the tail beyond `N`, so that `S_\infty(\gamma) = S_N(\gamma) + T_N(\gamma)` formally.

#### Proposition 2.2 (tail moments in the proxy model)

Under Model 2.1,

```math
E[T_N(\gamma)] = 0,
```

and

```math
E\!\left[|T_N(\gamma)|^2\right]
= \sum_{n > N} \frac{1}{n^2}
= \frac{1}{N} + O\!\left(\frac{1}{N^2}\right).
```

Moreover,

```math
E\!\left[|T_N(\gamma)|^4\right] \ll \frac{1}{N^2}.
```

#### Proof sketch

The first identity is immediate from centering. The second is the diagonal contribution. The fourth-moment bound follows from pairings and the estimate `\sum_{n>N} n^{-2} \asymp N^{-1}`.

### 2.4 White-noise interpretation for the residual

#### Proposition 2.3 (mean and variance of the centered residual)

Let

```math
\widetilde R_N(\gamma) := |S_\infty(\gamma)|^2 - |S_N(\gamma)|^2.
```

In Model 2.1,

```math
E[\widetilde R_N(\gamma)] = \frac{1}{N} + O\!\left(\frac{1}{N^2}\right),
```

and

```math
Var(\widetilde R_N(\gamma)) = O\!\left(\frac{1}{N}\right).
```

Hence the power-level noise floor is `O(1/N)`, while the amplitude-level noise floor is `O(N^{-1/2})`.

#### Proof sketch

Write `S_\infty = S_N + T_N`. Then

```math
\widetilde R_N = 2 \Re(S_N \overline{T_N}) + |T_N|^2.
```

The expectation of the linear term vanishes. The expectation of `|T_N|^2` is `\sum_{n>N} n^{-2}`. For the variance, use Cauchy-Schwarz and `E|S_N|^2 = \sum_{n \le N} n^{-2} = O(1)` together with `E|T_N|^2 = O(1/N)`.

### 2.5 No peaks above the noise floor

#### Proposition 2.4 (Chowla proxy implies flat background)

After the zero peaks are subtracted, the residual `R_N(\gamma)` is white-noise-like in the following sense:

- its mean is `O(1/N)` in power
- its variance is `O(1/N)` in power
- there is no additional deterministic spectral line above the `N^{-1}` background unless Chowla fails

This is the precise sense in which the residual is indistinguishable from white noise in the Chowla proxy.

#### Status

`CONDITIONAL` if one treats Chowla as the deterministic source of the proxy model.

### 2.6 What happens if Chowla is violated

Suppose there is a fixed shift `h \ge 1` and a nonzero bias `\epsilon` such that

```math
\sum_{n \le N-h} \mu(n)\mu(n+h) = \epsilon N + o(N).
```

Then the covariance structure of the power spectrum acquires an extra term.

#### Proposition 2.5 (spectral signature of a shift bias)

In the same proxy model, the additional contribution to the expected power is

```math
\Delta_h E[F_N(\gamma)]
= 2\epsilon \sum_{n \le N-h} \frac{\cos\!\big(\gamma \log(1+h/n)\big)}{n(n+h)}
 + \text{(higher-correlation terms)}.
```

Therefore

```math
\sup_{\gamma \in \mathbb R} |\Delta_h E[F_N(\gamma)]|
\le 2|\epsilon| \sum_{n \le N-h} \frac{1}{n(n+h)}
= \frac{2|\epsilon|}{h}\left(H_{N-h} + H_h - H_N\right),
```

where `H_m = \sum_{j \le m} j^{-1}` is the harmonic number.

Equivalently,

```math
\sup_{\gamma \in \mathbb R} |\Delta_h E[F_N(\gamma)]|
= \frac{2|\epsilon|}{h}\left(H_h + O(h/N)\right)
```

for fixed `h` and large `N`.

#### Proof sketch

Expand `|S_N(\gamma)|^2` and isolate the pairs `(n,n+h)`. The weight `1/(n(n+h))` is what survives after whitening. The phase factor is bounded by `1`, and the telescoping identity

```math
\frac{1}{n(n+h)} = \frac{1}{h}\left(\frac{1}{n} - \frac{1}{n+h}\right)
```

gives the harmonic-number formula.

#### Corollary 2.6 (detectability threshold)

If the power-level background noise is `\sigma_N^2 \asymp N^{-1}`, then a shift bias is detectable once

```math
|\epsilon| \gtrsim \frac{h}{2H_h} \cdot \frac{1}{N}.
```

If one instead works at the amplitude level, the threshold is `|\epsilon| \gtrsim \frac{h}{2H_h} N^{-1/2}`.

For fixed `h`, this is `\Theta(N^{-1})` in power and `\Theta(N^{-1/2})` in amplitude.

#### Remark 2.7

This is the correct detectability scale for the *pre-whitened* transform. For the unweighted transform, the same covariance bias typically produces an `O(\epsilon N)` line, which is where `N^{2-\epsilon}` thresholds arise.

## 3. Chowla versus the explicit formula

The explicit formula predicts deterministic peaks at the zero ordinates. Chowla predicts that, once those peaks are removed, the remaining spectral mass has no systematic structure beyond the white-noise floor.

### Proposition 3.1 (structural statement)

Assume RH and simple zeros, and assume the Chowla proxy model for the Mobius coefficients. Then:

1. The only persistent peaks in `F_N(\gamma)` are those produced by the zero ordinates `\gamma_k`.
2. The centered residual `R_N(\gamma)` has mean `O(1/N)` in power and variance `O(1/N)` in power.
3. Any further line spectrum in `R_N` is evidence of a Chowla violation.

#### Status

`CONDITIONAL`.

## 4. Sarnak, deterministic phases, and analytic orthogonality

### 4.1 A correction to the literal dynamical statement

Sarnak's conjecture is formulated for sequences of the form `f(T^n x)` where `(X,T)` is a zero-entropy topological dynamical system. The sequence

```math
a_n = n^{it} = e^{it \log n}
```

is a deterministic phase, but it is not a standard example of an orbit in a compact zero-entropy system. So the implication from Sarnak to `\sum_{n \le N} \mu(n)n^{it} = o(N)` is best read as a *philosophical analogue*, not as a literal theorem of Sarnak in the usual formulation.

### 4.2 The rigorous analytic substitute

#### Proposition 4.1 (classical orthogonality to Archimedean characters)

For each fixed `t \in \mathbb R`,

```math
\sum_{n \le N} \mu(n) n^{it} = o(N).
```

More precisely, by standard zero-free-region methods for `\zeta(s+it)` and partial summation,

```math
\sum_{n \le N} \mu(n) n^{it}
\ll_t N \exp(-c_t \sqrt{\log N})
```

for some `c_t > 0`.

#### Status

`PROVED` unconditionally.

#### Proof sketch

The Dirichlet series `\sum \mu(n)n^{-s} = 1/\zeta(s)` converges for `\Re s > 1` and continues analytically away from the zeros of `\zeta`. A zero-free region to the right of `\Re s = 1` gives cancellation in the partial sums, exactly as in Davenport-type bounds for twists of the Mobius function.

### 4.3 Relation to Sarnak

The theorem above is the Archimedean analogue of Mobius orthogonality to a deterministic phase. It plays the same role as:

- orthogonality to Dirichlet characters in the prime number theorem in arithmetic progressions
- orthogonality to low-complexity nilsequences and other zero-entropy systems in the Sarnak program

For compact rotations and their standard extensions, the Sarnak philosophy is consistent with this orthogonality; see Sarnak's lectures and subsequent work on Mobius disjointness.

### 4.4 Peak obstruction

Let

```math
\mathcal F_N(\gamma) := \left|\sum_{n \le N} \mu(n) n^{-i\gamma}\right|^2.
```

This is the unweighted Fourier analogue of `F_N`.

#### Theorem 4.2 (peak implies failure of orthogonality)

If for some fixed `\gamma_0 \in \mathbb R` and some `\epsilon > 0`,

```math
\mathcal F_N(\gamma_0) \ge C N^{2-\epsilon}
```

for infinitely many `N`, then

```math
\left|\sum_{n \le N} \mu(n) n^{-i\gamma_0}\right|
\ge \sqrt C\, N^{1-\epsilon/2}
```

infinitely often. In particular,

```math
\sum_{n \le N} \mu(n) n^{-i\gamma_0} \neq o(N),
```

so any Sarnak-type orthogonality statement for the deterministic phase `n^{i\gamma_0}` fails.

#### Proof

The first inequality is just the square root. The conclusion is immediate.

#### Remark 4.3

For the *weighted* spectroscope `F_N`, the correct absolute scale is `(log N)^2`, not `N^{2-\epsilon}`. So Theorem 4.2 should be read for the unweighted analogue.

## 5. Main theorem

The next statement is the cleanest rigorous version of the link between a spectral peak and Sarnak-style orthogonality.

#### Theorem 5.1 (spectral peak obstruction)

Fix `\gamma_0 \in \mathbb R`. Consider the unweighted transform

```math
\mathcal F_N(\gamma)
= \left|\sum_{n \le N} \mu(n) n^{-i\gamma}\right|^2.
```

Assume that `\gamma_0` is not the ordinate of any nontrivial zero of `\zeta(s)`.

If there exist `C > 0` and `\epsilon > 0` such that

```math
\mathcal F_N(\gamma_0) \ge C N^{2-\epsilon}
```

for infinitely many `N`, then the orthogonality relation

```math
\sum_{n \le N} \mu(n) n^{-i\gamma_0} = o(N)
```

fails. Consequently, any zero-entropy dynamical model generating the phase `n^{i\gamma_0}` would contradict the Sarnak disjointness philosophy.

#### Status

The logical implication from the peak to the failure of orthogonality is `PROVED`. The statement about a zero-entropy dynamical model is `CONDITIONAL` because the phase `n^{i\gamma_0}` is not a standard compact orbit in Sarnak's original setup.

#### Discussion: RH versus unconditionality

- The implication "large peak `\Rightarrow` failure of orthogonality" is unconditional.
- The location of the deterministic peaks at the zeta ordinates is conditional on the explicit formula and, for the clean critical-line picture, on RH plus simplicity of zeros.
- The orthogonality `\sum_{n \le N} \mu(n)n^{it} = o(N)` is unconditional by classical analytic number theory.

So the theorem is unconditional as an analytic obstruction, but the interpretation as a literal Sarnak statement is conditional on how one models the phase sequence dynamically.

## 6. Computational heuristics and the novel pieces

These are not theorems. They are useful numerical definitions.

### 6.1 The `\gamma^2` filter

Define a high-pass filter, for example

```math
\Phi_N(\gamma) := \frac{\gamma^2}{1+\gamma^2}\, R_N(\gamma).
```

This suppresses the low-frequency background coming from the `s=1` pole and emphasizes the ordinates away from the origin.

### 6.2 Local z-score

For a small window `I_\gamma = [\gamma-w,\gamma+w]`, define

```math
Z_N(\gamma) := \frac{R_N(\gamma) - \operatorname{median}_{u \in I_\gamma} R_N(u)}
{\operatorname{MAD}_{u \in I_\gamma} R_N(u)}.
```

This is a numerical outlier detector, not a theorem.

### 6.3 Farey `R(p)` connection

The Farey-discrepancy side of the story comes from the same explicit-formula machinery:

- `M(x)` is the common arithmetic input
- the Farey discrepancy can be expressed by Mobius inversion and summatory transforms of `M`
- the same residual decomposition can therefore be transferred to Farey-rank statistics such as the `R(p)` quantities used in the experiments

This is a plausible and useful bridge, but at present it should be treated as a computational conjecture rather than a proved theorem.

## 7. What is proved, what is conjectural, and what is computational

### PROVED

- the explicit formula for `M(x)` with residues at zeta zeros and the `-2` pole contribution
- the `1/n`-weighted transform is bounded by `(\log N)^2`
- the classical orthogonality `\sum_{n \le N} \mu(n)n^{it} = o(N)` for fixed `t`
- large peaks in the unweighted transform force failure of that orthogonality

### CONDITIONAL

- the clean peak picture on the critical line with peaks centered at `\gamma_k`
- the Chowla-to-white-noise interpretation for the residual
- the literal Sarnak-style interpretation of `n^{it}` as a zero-entropy orbit

### HEURISTIC / COMPUTATIONAL

- the `\gamma^2` filter
- the local z-score
- the Farey `R(p)` bridge
- peak-detection thresholds inferred from a proxy model rather than proved from Chowla
