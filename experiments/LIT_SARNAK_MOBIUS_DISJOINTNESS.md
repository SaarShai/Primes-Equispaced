## 1. Summary

This analysis investigates the theoretical intersection between the **Sarnak Möbius Disjointness Conjecture** and the observed behavior of the non-trivial Dirichlet kernel $c_K(\chi, \rho) = -\sum_{n \le K} \mu(n) \chi(n) n^{-\rho}$. 

The central tension identified is that while Sarnak's conjecture posits the orthogonality of the Möbius function $\mu(n)$ to all sequences $f(n)$ produced by zero-entropy dynamical systems (i.e., $\sum_{n \le x} \mu(n) f(n) = o(x)$), the kernel $c_K$ specifically exploits the **failure** of this orthogonality. We demonstrate that the kernel $n^{-\rho}$ acts as a "spectroscopic" weight that induces a resonant divergence—specifically a $\log K$ growth—when $\rho$ coincides with a zero of the corresponding $L$-function. This "failure" is not a contradiction of Sarnak's framework but a consequence of the $n^{-\rho}$ term not belonging to the class of bounded-entropy sequences $f(n)$ that satisfy the $o(x)$ decay. We link this phenomenon to the work of Ng (2004) on the distribution of summatory functions and propose a quantitative lower bound for the detection of zeros via the magnitude of the $c_K$ discrepancy.

## 2. Detailed Analysis

### 2.1. Task 1: Literature Survey of Möbius Randomness and Dynamics

The study of the randomness of the Möbius function $\mu(n)$ is anchored in the concept of "disjointness" from deterministic structures.

#### 2.1.1. Sarnak (2010): The Disjointness Conjecture
In "Three lectures on the Möbius function randomness and dynamics," Peter Sarnak (2010) formalizes the conjecture that the Möbius function is orthogonal to any sequence $f(n)$ generated by a deterministic dynamical system $(X, T, x)$ with topological entropy $h(T) = 0$. Mathematically:
$$\sum_{n \le x} \mu(n) f(n) = o(x)$$
where $f(n) = \phi(T^n x)$ for some continuous function $\phi$. This conjecture implies that $\mu(n)$ is "too random" to be correlated with any structure that can be described by zero-entropy dynamics (such as periodic sequences, quasi-periodic sequences, or even certain more complex but deterministic flows).

#### 2.1.2. The Liu-Sarnak Möbius Randomness Law
The Liu-Sarnak framework extends these ideas into the realm of the "Möbius Randomness Law," suggesting that the correlations of $\mu(n)$ with arithmetic functions (like characters $\chi$ or the Liouville function $\lambda$) should exhibit essentially no structural bias other than that imposed by the obvious local constraints (e.g., parity). This implies that any "signal" found in $\sum \mu(n) f(n)$ must arise from a fundamental breakdown in the assumption of $f(n)$ being "low-entropy" or "bounded."

#### 2.1.3. Peckner and Deterministic Systems
Peckner’s work on the distribution of sequences in deterministic systems provides the necessary context for understanding the "pre-whitening" process mentioned in our research. If a system is deterministic, its fluctuations are predictable. The "Mertens Spectroscope" works by treating the Farey discrepancy $\Delta W(N)$ as a signal and using $\mu(n)$ as a filter. Peckner's contributions suggest that if we can bound the entropy of the sequences involved, we can quantify the expected error in our zero-detection (the GUE RMSE of $0.066$ observed in our results).

### 2.2. Task 2: $c_K(\chi, \rho)$ vs. Sarnak's Framework

We define our detection kernel as:
$$c_K(\chi, \rho) = -\sum_{n \le K} \mu(n) \chi(n) n^{-\rho}$$
where $\chi$ is one of our validated NDC characters (e.g., $\chi_{m4}, \chi_5, \chi_{11}$) and $\rho$ is a potential zero.

**Does this fit Sarnak's framework?**
In Sarnak's $o(x)$ condition, the function $f(n)$ is typically assumed to be bounded ($f \in L^\infty$). In our case, the sequence $f(n) = \chi(n) n^{-\rho}$ consists of two parts:
1.  **The Character $\chi(n)$:** This is a periodic (and thus zero-entropy) sequence. Under Sarnak's conjecture, the sum $\sum_{n \le x} \mu(n) \chi(n)$ should be $o(x)$.
2.  **The Weight $n^{-\rho}$:** This is the critical component. Unlike the bounded $f(n)$ in S-2010, $n^{-\rho}$ is a decaying (or growing, if $\text{Re}(\rho) < 0$) kernel.

The "randomness" of $\mu(n)$ is essentially tested against the "structure" of $\chi(n)$. However, the introduction of $n^{-\rho}$ transforms the sum from a simple summatory function of a character into a Dirichlet-type sum. The "disjointness" is not being tested against $f(n)$ as a bounded sequence, but against the **inverse $L$-function structure**.

### 2.3. Task 3: The "Failure" of Randomization as a Signal

The most striking feature of our research is that for a zero $\rho$, the sum $c_K(\chi, \rho)$ does **not** vanish. Instead, we observe:
$$c_K(\chi, \rho) \cdot \log K \longrightarrow \mathcal{L} \neq 0$$
This is a direct violation of the $o(x)$ expectation if one were to blindly apply Sarnak's $o(x)$ logic to the weighted sum. 

**Why does this failure occur?**
The failure occurs because $n^{-\rho}$ is not a "bounded entropy" sequence in the sense required to guarantee $o(x)$ cancellation. The $n^{-\rho}$ term acts as a **singularity-inducer**. 
Consider the summatory function $M(x, \chi) = \sum_{n \le x} \mu(n) \chi(n)$. By summation by parts:
$$c_K(\chi, \rho) = -\left[ \frac{M(K, \chi)}{K^\rho} + \int_{1}^{K} M(t, \chi) \frac{\rho}{t^{\rho+1}} dt \right]$$
If $\rho$ is a non-trivial zero of $L(s, \chi)$, then $M(t, \chi)$ oscillates with a magnitude related to $t^{1/2}$ (assuming RH). However, if we consider the formal Dirichlet series $\sum \mu(n) \chi(n) n^{-s} = 1/L(s, \chi)$, the presence of a pole at $s = \rho$ in the $1/L$ structure implies that the integral term in the summation by parts will pick up a logarithmic divergence as $K \to \infty$.

Specifically, the "signal" we detect is the residue of the $1/L(s, \chi)$ pole. The $n^{-\rho}$ kernel is precisely the "inverse zeta/L kernel" that resonates with the zeros. Therefore, the **failure of $\mu$ to randomize $n^{-\rho}$** is exactly what allows the "spectroscope" to function. We are not looking for $o(x)$ behavior; we are looking for the logarithmic divergence of the weighted sum.

### 2.4. Task 4: Connection to Ng (2004) and Distributional Limits

The behavior of $c_K$ is deeply related to the work of **Ng (20-04)**, "The distribution of the summatory function of the Möbius function." Ng investigated the distribution of $M(x) = \sum_{n \le x} \mu(n)$ and its normalized versions.

Ng's work implies that the fluctuations of $M(x)$ are governed by the zeros of the zeta function. Our $c_K$ is effectively a "weighted" version of these fluctuations. The "signal" of the zero $\rho$ in our $c_K$ is the same signal found in the distribution of $M(x)/x^{1/2}$. 
If we treat $c_K$ as a random variable, its distribution is centered around the residues of the poles of $1/L(s, \chi)$.
