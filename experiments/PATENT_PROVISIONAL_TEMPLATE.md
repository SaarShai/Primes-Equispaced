### **Requirements for a US Provisional Patent Application**  
1. **Description of the Invention**  
   - Must be **detailed and complete** to enable someone skilled in the art to replicate the invention.  
   - Include:  
     - **Technical background** (problem to be solved).  
     - **Invention details** (how it works, steps, components, or algorithms).  
     - **Examples** (specific implementations or use cases).  
     - **Technical effect** (e.g., improved efficiency, novel outcome).  
   - For **mathematical methods**: Must show a **technical application** (e.g., solving a real-world problem, optimizing a system, or enabling a technical process). Pure mathematical theories are not patentable.  

2. **Drawings (if applicable)**  
   - Required if the invention includes physical components, processes, or systems.  
   - For **mathematical methods**, drawings are **optional** but can help clarify implementation (e.g., flowcharts, diagrams of systems using the method).  

3. **Cover Sheet**  
   - Include:  
     - Inventor(s) and applicant(s) names, addresses, and contact information.  
     - Title of the invention.  
     - Filing date.  

4. **Filing Fee**  
   - **Small entity fee**: **$60** (not $20; the user may have a typo).  
   - **Large entity fee**: $120.  

5. **No Claims Needed**  
   - **Provisional applications** do **not require claims**. However, the **specification** must include a **detailed description** that could support claims in a subsequent non-provisional application.  

---

### **Provisional Priority Period**  
- **12 months** from the filing date of the provisional application.  
- This period allows the applicant to file a **non-provisional patent application** to claim the same priority date.  

---

### **Description for a Mathematical Method Patent**  
To qualify for a patent, a mathematical method must demonstrate **technical application** (e.g., solving a technical problem). The description should:  
1. **Define the problem** (e.g., inefficiency in data processing, security vulnerabilities).  
2. **Explain the mathematical method** (algorithm, formula, or process).  
3. **Show implementation** (e.g., how it is applied to a system, device, or process).  
4. **Highlight technical effects** (e.g., reduced computation time, enhanced security, or improved performance).  
5. **Include examples** (specific use cases or scenarios where the method is applied).  
6. **Avoid pure theory**: Focus on **practical application** (e.g., a mathematical model used in engineering, finance, or healthcare).  

**Note**: Pure mathematical formulas or abstract concepts (e.g., "a method of adding numbers") are **not patentable**. The invention must have a **technical effect** or **practical application**.  

---

### **Key Takeaways**  
- **Provisional applications** are a cost-effective way to secure a priority date.  
- **Mathematical methods** require **technical context** to meet patentability standards.  
- Always ensure the description is **detailed enough** to support future claims and enable replication.
