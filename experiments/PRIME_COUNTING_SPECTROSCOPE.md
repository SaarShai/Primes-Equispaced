This is a profound observation that touches on the heart of "spectral" number theory. You are essentially comparing two different "frequency-domain" representations of the distribution of primes: one that is **purely harmonic** (the Chebyshev $\psi$ function) and one that is **modulated by the zeta-derivative** (the prime-counting error $M(p)$).

To determine which has a better **Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR)**, we must define what constitutes the "Signal" and what constitutes the "Noise" in the context of an explicit formula.

### 1. The Signal: Amplitude of the Peaks
In both spectroscopes, the "signal" is the detectable oscillation at a frequency $\gamma$ (the imaginary part of a non-trivial zero $\rho = \beta + i\gamma$).

*   **Spectroscope (b) [$\psi(x) - x$]:** The amplitude of the oscillation at $\gamma$ is approximately:
    $$A_\gamma \approx \frac{1}{|\rho|} \approx \frac{1}{\gamma}$$
    This is a "pure" harmonic decay. Each zero contributes a predictable, decaying impulse to the spectrum.
*   **Spectroscope (a) [$M(p)$]:** The amplitude of the oscillation is:
    $$A_\gamma \approx \frac{1}{|\gamma \cdot \zeta'(\rho)|}$$
    Here, the amplitude is "modulated" by the value of the derivative of the zeta function at the zero.

**The Signal Verdict:** If we look strictly at the *strength* of a single peak, **(a) has the potential for much higher signal strength.** If $\zeta'(\rho)$ is very small (which occurs when zeros are "crowded" or near-multiple, though we assume they are simple), the peak in $M(p)$ becomes massive. However, this strength is highly unpredictable.

### 2. The Noise: The "Background" Interference
In a spectrum derived from a sum of infinite oscillations, "noise" is not external thermal noise; it is **interference noise** (or "spectral leakage") caused by the cumulative tails of all other frequencies in the sum.

*   **In $\psi(x)-x$:** The "noise" is the sum of the tails: $\sum_{\gamma \neq \gamma_0} \frac{1}{\gamma}$. Because the weights decay monotonically and predictably as $1/\gamma$, the background is a relatively "smooth" interference pattern. The "power" in the tail is convergent in a squared sense ($\sum 1/\gamma^2 < \infty$), meaning the total energy of the "noise" is bounded and well-behaved.
*   **In $M(p)$:** The "noise" is $\sum_{\gamma \neq \gamma_0} \frac{1}{\gamma |\zeta'(\rho)|}$. This is much more dangerous. The values of $|\zeta'(\rho)|$ can be extremely small. This means the "background" is not smooth; it is a "jittery" sea of unpredictable, high-amplitude fluctuations. A single zero with a very small $\zeta'(\rho)$ could create a "noise spike" that masks the signal of the frequency you are actually trying to measure.

### 3. Comparison: The SNR Analysis

| Feature | (b) $\psi(x)-x$ (Chebyshev) | (a) $M(p)$ (Prime-counting error) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Peak Amplitude** | Predictable ($1/\gamma$) | Volatile ($1/\gamma |\zeta'|$) |
| **Spectral Smoothness** | High (Harmonic decay) | Low (Stochastic modulation) |
| **Noise Source** | Deterministic interference | Unpredictable "Spike" interference |
| **Reliability** | High (Every zero is visible) | Low (Zeros can be buried by $\zeta'$ spikes) |

**Conclusion on SNR:**
The **$\psi(x) - x$ spectroscope has a better SNR.**

While the $M(p)$ spectroscope can produce "louder" peaks (higher signal), it simultaneously produces much "louder" noise (higher variance in the background). In signal processing terms, the $\psi$ function is a **Stationary Process** in terms of its spectral weight, whereas the $M(p)$ function is a **Non-stationary/Impulsive Process**.

The $\psi(x)$ function acts like a clean sine-wave synthesis where the amplitude is strictly controlled by the frequency. The $M(p)$ function acts like a spectrum where some frequencies are randomly amplified by a massive gain factor ($1/|\zeta'|$), making the "background" chaotic. 

**Final Verdict:** For a reliable "detection" of the zeros, the **$\psi(x)-x$ spectroscope** is superior because it minimizes the "interference noise" generated by the erratic behavior of the zeta-derivative.
