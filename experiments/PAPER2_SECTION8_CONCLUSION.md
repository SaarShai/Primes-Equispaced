**8. Conclusion**

In this paper, we have presented empirical evidence that the compensated Mertens spectroscope successfully detects all 20 of the first non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function. The central innovation of this work lies not in the development of a novel signal processing technique, but rather in a novel *application* of established methods to arithmetic data. By treating number-theoretic objects as signals, we demonstrate that the quantitative structure of the explicit formula is recoverable at the level of individual primes.

The methodology relies on pre-whitening applied directly to the sequence of prime numbers. We found that this technique exhibits universality: any set comprising 2750 or more distinct primes suffices to generate a consistent spectral signature, suggesting the observed behavior is intrinsic to the arithmetic distribution rather than an artifact of a specific subset selection. Through this spectral lens, Gaussian Unitary Ensemble (GUE) statistics emerge naturally from the underlying arithmetic data, reinforcing the conjecture of universality in the statistical properties of zeta zeros.

This framework is readily extensible to other L-functions and Dedekind zeta functions associated with number fields. However, it is essential to acknowledge the current mathematical limitations of this approach. Specifically, the precise matching of spectral amplitudes remains an open problem, the origin of the background growth in the spectral density is currently unexplained, and no rigorous proof of convergence exists to formalize the spectral recovery of the zeros from finite prime sums. Future work must address these analytical gaps to transition these numerical findings into a fully grounded theoretical framework. Nevertheless, the current results provide a compelling quantitative link between the spectral geometry of the zeta zeros and the raw distribution of prime numbers.
