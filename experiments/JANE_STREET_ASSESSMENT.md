### The Neural Network Reverse-Engineering Challenge (arXiv:2602.19845)

The "I Dropped a Neural Net" paper tackles recovering shuffled layers of a residual network. The search space is ~(48!)^2. The solution uses:
- Dynamic isometry / diagonal dominance to pair input/output projections
- Frobenius norm proxies + hill-climbing for layer ordering

**Number theory plays zero role.** This is purely about neural network training dynamics and matrix structure. Our Farey expertise would not help here.

### Verdict on puzzles

**Stretch.** Jane Street puzzles test broad mathematical problem-solving, not number-theoretic depth. Farey expertise would occasionally help (maybe 1 in 20 puzzles), but is not a differentiator. Their hiring puzzles test general quantitative reasoning, not specialist knowledge.

## 2. Farey for Neural Network Architectures

### Existing work using number theory in ML

There IS a real research thread here, but it goes in the opposite direction from what we might expect:

- **QMC Training (Mishra & Rusch, SIAM 2021):** Low-discrepancy sequences (Sobol, Halton) as training point sets. Proven O(N^{-1}) generalization gap vs O(N^{-1/2}) for Monte Carlo. Real speedups: 1-2 orders of magnitude for PDE surrogates.
- **Higher-Order QMC-DNN (Longo et al., SIAM 2021):** Achieves quadratic (or arbitrary-order) decay of generalization error using extrapolated polynomial lattice points.
- **NeuroLDS (Van Huffel, arXiv 2510.03745):** Neural networks that LEARN to generate low-discrepancy sequences. Uses sinusoidal features inspired by radical-inverse digits.
- **Keller & Van Keirsbilck (MCQMC 2020):** ANNs generated by low-discrepancy sequences for weight initialization.

### Assessment of each proposed idea

**(a) Farey-scheduled learning rate annealing**
- HONEST ASSESSMENT: Gimmick. Learning rate schedules (cosine, warm restarts, cyclical) are chosen for optimization landscape reasons, not sampling uniformity. Placing LR changes at Farey positions in [0,1] has no theoretical motivation. The optimizer does not care that your schedule hits equidistributed rationals.
- Existing work that IS relevant: cyclical LR (Smith 2017), cosine annealing (Loshchilov & Hutter). These work for dynamical reasons, not number-theoretic ones.

**(b) Farey-based weight initialization**
- PARTIALLY LEGITIMATE. Keller & Van Keirsbilck (2020) literally did this -- using low-discrepancy sequences for weight initialization. However, the standard approaches (Kaiming/He, Xavier/Glorot) are designed around variance preservation through layers, which is the actual bottleneck. Equidistribution of initial values is a secondary concern at best.
- Would be a minor refinement, not a breakthrough.

**(c) Farey quantization for model compression**
- MOST PROMISING of the four, but still incremental. Standard quantization uses uniform grids (INT8, INT4). Non-uniform quantization to Farey fractions F_N would give a principled rational approximation scheme with bounded denominators. The mediant property could guide rounding decisions.
- However: current SOTA quantization (GPTQ, AWQ, SqueezeLLM) already uses non-uniform schemes tuned to weight distributions. Farey quantization would be theoretically elegant but likely inferior to data-driven approaches.
- Potential niche: hardware that natively computes with small-denominator rationals.

**(d) Farey-guided dropout**
- HONEST ASSESSMENT: Gimmick. Dropout works by breaking co-adaptation of neurons. The positions where you drop are intentionally random. Structured dropout exists (DropBlock, spatial dropout) but the structure comes from the data topology, not number theory. Farey-positioned drops have no theoretical motivation.

### Overall ML verdict

The real connection between number theory and ML is through **quasi-Monte Carlo methods for training**, which is a legitimate and active research area. But this uses Sobol/Halton sequences, not Farey sequences specifically. The Farey angle would be a repackaging of existing QMC ideas with a different sequence family. It could work for a paper, but would need to demonstrate concrete advantages over Sobol/Halton, which are already well-optimized for high dimensions.

## 3. Machine Learning at Jane Street

### What ML problems they work on

From their hiring pages:
- Predictive modeling for trading strategies (deep learning on market data)
- Signal extraction from extremely noisy, nonstationary financial data
- Large-scale training on tens of thousands of GPUs
- Low-latency inference for real-time trading
- Multi-agent competitive environment modeling

### Where number theory could genuinely help

**Honest answer: mostly it cannot.** Their ML problems are about:
1. Feature engineering from market microstructure data
2. Handling nonstationarity and regime changes
3. Training efficiency at scale
4. Latency optimization

Number theory could marginally help with:
- **Rational approximation of price levels:** Prices cluster at psychologically round numbers. Stern-Brocot/Farey structure could model this, but behavioral finance already handles it.
- **QMC for Monte Carlo pricing:** This is real and used in finance (Sobol sequences for option pricing). But Jane Street likely already uses this -- it is standard quant finance.
- **Equidistribution tests for time series:** Checking whether trading signals are uniformly distributed. Weyl/Farey discrepancy tests could work here but are not better than standard statistical tests.

### Farey equidistribution and financial time series

**No established literature connects these.** I searched specifically for this. The equidistribution theory of Farey sequences lives in ergodic theory (horocycle flows, SL(2,R)), not in finance. The theoretical machinery is beautiful but has no natural financial interpretation.

**The honest summary:** The number-theory-to-ML pipeline runs through quasi-Monte Carlo, and that field already exists and uses different sequences (Sobol, Halton, lattice rules). Farey sequences are not standard QMC tools because they live in 1D and don't extend naturally to high dimensions -- the exact regime where QMC methods shine. Our Farey expertise is a hammer looking for nails in ML-land.

The one genuinely interesting angle: **Farey quantization as a principled non-uniform weight compression scheme**, with a paper framing it as "number-theoretic model compression." But this is a C1-level contribution at best (minor novelty, insufficient for a strong publication on its own).
